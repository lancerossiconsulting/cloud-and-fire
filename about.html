Who We Are

Rooted in Scripture. Grounded in Research. Built for Your Church.

A Christian fellowship for church leaders committed to the hard, holy work of listening well.

Our Mission

Flint Hills Cloud & Fire Fellowship exists to form church leaders in the practice of structured listening — equipping them to hear what their congregations are actually carrying and to respond with pastoral care that is grounded, not guessed.

We work at the intersection of two deep traditions: the Church's centuries-long practice of communal discernment, and three decades of peer-reviewed research in structured public dialogue. Neither tradition alone is sufficient. Together, they give church leaders something durable: a practice, not just a method; formation, not just a technique.

"Understand this, my dear brothers and sisters: You must all be quick to listen, slow to speak, and slow to get angry."

The Name

The name comes from Exodus 13. When Israel left Egypt and entered the wilderness, God guided the whole people — not just its leaders — with a pillar of cloud by day and fire by night. The pillar went ahead of them: communal guidance for a crossing no one had made before. And it did not lift when the light failed. The presence that led by day is the same presence that burned through the dark.

That image names what this fellowship aspires to be. Cloud: the presence of the Holy Spirit over a community that listens — with the mind of Christ, seeking first to understand, making room for the revelation that comes only when God's people truly hear one another. Fire: light for the night. Night comes to every congregation — seasons when the way forward is hard to see — and a church that has learned to listen with the Spirit is not left to wander in it. Its path is illuminated, and the whole body moves forward together.

What We Believe About Listening

Listening is a spiritual discipline

The discernment tradition of the Church has always held that listening to one another and listening to God are not competing activities. Structured conversation, held prayerfully, is a form of communal formation.

Design matters

Not all conversation is equal. Research consistently shows that how a conversation is structured determines whether it builds understanding or hardens conflict. Good facilitation is an act of pastoral stewardship.

The congregation knows things the pastor doesn't

Surveys and hallway conversations miss what people are actually carrying. Structured dialogue surfaces the grief, doubt, hope, and conflict that live beneath the surface of ordinary church life.

The local church is irreplaceable

No civic institution reaches as deeply into community life as the congregation. A church that listens well becomes a trusted anchor — for its members and for the wider community it serves.

Where We're From

The fellowship is rooted in Manhattan, Kansas — a small city in the Flint Hills, home to Kansas State University, Fort Riley, and a network of congregations that spans the full range of American Christian tradition. The Flint Hills have a way of asking honest questions. The land is open, the seasons are demanding, and the communities here know what it means to depend on one another.

That context shapes what the fellowship is for: not a conference program or a parachurch add-on, but a practice built for and by the churches of this place, offered to any congregation willing to do the work of listening well.
